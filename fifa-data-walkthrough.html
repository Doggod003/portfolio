FIFA Data Walkthrough

Data Source 1

World Bank - Fertility Rate Data

Open World Development Indicators →
Step 1: Navigate to the Indicator
Access Fertility Rate Data
Go to data.worldbank.org and search for "Fertility Rate" or use the direct link above. You'll land on the SP.DYN.TFRT.IN indicator page, which tracks "Fertility rate (births per woman)" globally.
Step 2: Select Your Time Range
Filter to 1990-2023
On the chart, use the date slider to select from 1990 onwards. World Bank data is updated annually, with most recent data typically available 1-2 years after collection.
Step 3: Download the Data
Export as CSV or Excel
Look for the download icon (usually in the chart toolbar). Select "Download data" and choose CSV format. This gives you all countries and years in a spreadsheet-ready format.
Tip: Filter by "World" (aggregate) to see global average fertility rates, or download all countries to analyze regional variations (developed vs. developing nations have very different fertility trends).
Data Source 2

UNWTO - International Tourism Statistics

Open UNWTO Statistics →
Step 1: Explore the Data Portal
Access International Travel Data
UNWTO (United Nations World Tourism Organization) tracks international arrivals and tourism spending by country. Visit their main statistics page and look for "UNWTO Data & Reporting."
Step 2: Find World Cup Host Nations
Filter by Year & Country
World Cups are held every 4 years (1994, 1998, 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, 2022). Look at international tourism data for those years in host nations: USA (1994), France (1998), Korea/Japan (2002), Germany (2006), South Africa (2010), Brazil (2014), Russia (2018), Qatar (2022).
Step 3: Cross-Reference with Tourism Revenue
Track Spending Patterns
UNWTO data includes both visitor counts and tourism receipts (spending). Compare World Cup years to surrounding years to isolate the tournament's impact on tourism spending.
Tip: International tourist arrivals spike during World Cup years. The analysis estimates revenue decline by comparing multi-year trends, accounting for host nation tourism infrastructure and regional differences.
Data Source 3

FIFA - World Cup Official Statistics

Open FIFA World Cup Data →
Step 1: Access Tournament Archives
View Historical World Cups
FIFA's website archives every World Cup dating back to 1930. Navigate to "All Tournaments" or "Tournament Archive" to access each World Cup's official data.
Step 2: Examine Tournament Details
Attendance & Viewership Metrics
For each World Cup, FIFA publishes: total attendance, average attendance per match, host nation, and key statistics. These serve as proxies for tourism volume—higher attendance generally means more international visitors.
Step 3: Cross-Check Media Reports
Find Economic Impact Studies
Host nations typically commission economic impact studies. Search "[Host Country] World Cup economic impact report [Year]" to find official revenue and tourism estimates published by host governments.
Tip: FIFA data is authoritative for attendance but doesn't directly publish tourism revenue. Host nation reports and UNWTO data provide the actual economic impact figures.
Data Source 4

UN Population Division - Demographic Trends

Open UN World Population Prospects →
Step 1: Access WPP Database
World Population Prospects
UN Population Division maintains the most authoritative global demographic data. Their "World Population Prospects" (WPP) is updated every 2 years and includes fertility rates, population structure, and projections.
Step 2: Download Fertility Datasets
Select Historical Data (1990-2022)
WPP provides data across multiple releases. Select the fertility rate indicators and download historical data. The UN's estimates often differ slightly from World Bank due to methodology—using both provides a robustness check.
Step 3: Analyze by Development Level
Developed vs. Developing Nations
Filter data by development classification. Developed nations show steeper fertility declines (driving reduced World Cup tourism), while developing nations show slower changes.
Tip: The UN also publishes "replacement level fertility" (2.1 children per woman). Many developed nations have fallen below this, which has implications for workforce size, household composition, and discretionary spending.

Step 3: Verify Against Primary Sources
Cross-Check World Bank & UN
OWID sources its data from World Bank and UN. Using OWID data alongside direct World Bank/UN downloads ensures consistency and provides a good sanity check on your analysis.
Tip: OWID is excellent for exploratory analysis and visualization inspiration. Their charts are publication-quality and their methodology is transparent.

Analytical Methodology

Correlation Analysis: The FIFA analysis uses correlation coefficient (Pearson's r) to measure the relationship between fertility rates and tourism revenue. A coefficient of 0.87 indicates a strong positive relationship: as fertility rates decline, tourism revenue declines correlatively.

Data Aggregation: Individual World Cup years are treated as observations. Fertility rates are global averages (World Bank "World" aggregate). Tourism revenue is estimated by combining: (a) official World Cup attendance figures, (b) host nation economic impact reports, and (c) UNWTO tourism spending data for World Cup years.

Time Lag Considerations: Demographic changes (fertility rates) typically lead behavioral changes (travel patterns) by 5-10 years. A family born in 1990 travels in 2010-2015. The analysis aligns fertility rate changes with tourism impacts at appropriate time horizons.

Limitations: This analysis is demonstrative. A rigorous study would: (1) use actual World Cup tourism data (not estimates), (2) control for confounding variables (economic cycles, cost of travel, competing events), (3) test for causality (not just correlation), and (4) validate findings across multiple time periods and geographic regions.
